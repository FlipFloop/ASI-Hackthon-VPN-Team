A. Rerouting algorithms (the headline)

  1. No-fly-zone reroute — visibility graph + Dijkstra

  What: draw a keep-out box → every flight crossing it flies around (or is grounded if its
  origin/destination is inside).
  How: for each affected flight it finds where the route enters/exits the box, builds a tiny
  visibility graph (the entry point, exit point, and the 4 slightly-inflated corners of the
  box), connects any two nodes whose straight line doesn't clip the box (Liang–Barsky
  segment-box clip test), and runs Dijkstra for the shortest way around. The detour is
  stitched back into the original route.
  Lives in: app.js → rerouteAround / shortestAround / segHitsBox.
  Demo line: "Draw a box over Chicago — every flight instantly reroutes around it,
  shortest-path."

  2. Plan-around-weather — reroute, else hold

  What: the forecast version — a flight that foresees storms on its path flies around them,
  or holds if it can't.
  How: storms are turned into keep-out boxes (see D2); for each weather-affected flight it
  chains the reroute above across every storm box on its path. If the destination is under
  the storm or it's boxed in → it's classified hold.
  Lives in: app.js → planAroundWeather / applyWxPlan.
  Demo line: "Turn on Plan-around-weather: 75 reroute around the storms, 71 must hold —
  here's the fuel bill."

  ▎ Note for accuracy in the demo: the in-browser router is a fast visibility-graph
  ▎ approximation. The toolkit ships a heavier A* router (autoroute.py) that searches a
  ▎ lat/lon grid with tunable cost weights (distance + traffic congestion + a safety buffer
  ▎ around the hazard + a turn-smoothness penalty). The viz reuses that toolkit's cost model
  ▎ but not its A* — say "grid A* is the production router; the map uses a fast equivalent."

  B. Cost models (what every reroute is priced with)

  3. Detour fuel cost — ported from the toolkit

  What: turns extra nautical miles into extra fuel (kg) → $ → CO₂.
  How: extra_kg = extra_nm / speed × burn, $ = kg / 3.04 × fuel_price, CO₂ = kg × 3.16. The
  burn rate comes from a piecewise-linear curve keyed on cruise speed (turboprop ~350 kg/hr →
  widebody ~3,300 kg/hr — speed is the only aircraft-size signal in the data).
  Lives in: app.js → priceDetour / burnKgHr (mirrors holdcost.burn_kghr +
  autoroute._package).
  Demo line: "Same constants as the toolkit, so the map and the algorithm agree to the
  dollar."

  4. Live fuel price

  What: the price driving every cost number is the real market price, not a constant.
  How: serve.py /api/fuel proxies ULSD/NY-Harbor front-month futures (the jet-fuel hedging
  proxy) + a jet differential; the page auto-pulls it on load, with a slider to override.
  Demo line: "It pulled $3.67/gal live — slide fuel up and watch every decision change."

  C. Congestion & delay (air-vs-ground decision)

  5. AAR arrival metering — single-server queue / Ration-by-Schedule

  What: an airport can only land so many planes per hour (the Acceptance Rate). Reroutes
  bunch arrivals → a queue forms → planes must wait.
  How: sort arrivals by ETA, meter them one slot every 3600/AAR seconds (FAA
  Ration-by-Schedule); the gap between your ETA and your slot is your hold.
  Lives in: app.js → meterSlots / meterArrivals (ported from holdcost.py).

  6. CDM substitution — cost-optimal slot swap, decided by live fuel

  What: this is the "circle vs stay on the ground" decision. A held plane can wait in the air
  (circling burns cruise fuel — expensive, and past a 45-min reserve it's a diversion risk)
  or at the gate (burns ~nothing). CDM lets an airline reshuffle its own flights across its
  own slots so the cheap/gate-holdable planes absorb the wait and the expensive jets land on
  time.
  How: within each carrier, airborne flights grab the earliest feasible slots (they're
  already burning), gate-holdable flights take the latest; price each hold by burn × live
  fuel.
  Lives in: app.js → assignCheapest / holdFuelKg.
  Demo line: "Same total delay, fair within each airline — but it cuts diversion risk 31→5
  and saves real money, because it's priced in live fuel."

  7. GDP (Ground Delay Program) — iterative sector-capacity rationing

  What: the "GDP" scenario — precomputed ground delays that keep sectors under capacity.
  How: loop — find the worst over-demand sector, give ground delays in schedule order so it
  stops overflowing, repeat until no sector is over (airborne flights can't be delayed, so
  some are unresolvable).
  Lives in: viz/gdp.py (offline) → gdp.json.

  D. Weather algorithms

  8. Weather conflict detection (cf)

  What: which flights actually hit impassable weather.
  How: offline, sample each flight's position at every 15-min forecast strip; it's "in
  weather" when local reflectivity ≥ 40 dBZ AND echo-top ≥ the flight's altitude (can't climb
  over it).
  Lives in: preprocess.py → per-flight cf strip intervals.

  9. Storm-reduced sector capacity

  What: storms shrink how many planes a sector can safely hold.
  How: per sector/timestep, severity = blend of intensity (maxdBZ−40)/15 and coverage (heavy
  cells/6); capacity loss = 0.30 + 0.70·severity (30–100%); effective capacity = normal × (1
  − loss). (That LOW_122 ⛈ 9/4 over you saw is this in action.)
  Lives in: preprocess_sectors.py compute_stormcap → effCap/stormRed in app.js.

  10. Storm keep-out boxes (for plan-around-weather)

  What: turning the storm raster into shapes a router can avoid.
  How: take all ≥40 dBZ cells per strip, cluster them on a coarse grid via connected
  components (flood-fill), emit each cluster's padded bounding box.
  Lives in: preprocess_sectors.py compute_storm_boxes → storm_boxes.json.
